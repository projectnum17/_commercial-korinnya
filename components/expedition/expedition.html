about My name is George, some journalists call me "Jungle George", and in fact, this is a pretty apt description of how I work with photography at the front.
It all started with a large-scale russian offensive on the territory of Ukraine.

I was in Lviv, and it immediately became clear that something globally important and terrible was happening And every shot would not be the same as before.

06.02.2022 LVIV Nastia Horokh and their cat a couple who evacuated from Vinnytsia. The moment of deciding that Nastia should be sent farther away from the war to the Czech Republic and understanding the long separation

I had been volunteering and taking pictures at the Lviv railway station for a week when a Chilean journalist asked me if I could help him catch a train to Kyiv. To Kyiv, where thousands of people are leaving to save themselves, their children, and their animals. It inspired me, and I realized that I wanted to go there, to a place where something even more important was happening, and that I had to capture it.

23.03.2022 Kyiv Members of the KOSMOSTABIR Cultural Organization who have retrained to make armored jets and tank hedgehogs relax after a day's work

The next day I was already in Kyiv, me and my backpack. I lived and worked with the cultural community KOSMOSTABIR, which started sewing tile carriers and cooking anti-tank hedgehogs. We were traveling, carrying out missions, and keeping the cultural front.

In the first days of the liberation of the Kyiv region, I got on a volunteer bus and went to Borodyanka. Three kilometers away, my small homeland is the village of Pylypovychi, where my grandparents stayed. But nothing compares to the moment I entered Borodyanka: the air was yellow with dust and heavy with the realization of what had happened here. I spent three days with the rescuers, searching the village for footage, accompanied by constant flashbacks from my childhood.

This is where my expeditions began. In fact, there was only one, just a very long one.

The next time, together with a Belarusian journalist Dmytro Galko, who has been living in Ukraine for a long time, we studied the ways of attack in Chernihiv region, and it took about 3 weeks.

27.04.2022 ZDVIZHIVKA, BUCHACHASKY DISTRICT, KYIV REGION Grandma tells the story of how the Russians came into the basement and took away their last food. Her son, not in the military, was held hostage in the church for several weeks

Then I spent a month in Kharkiv a month in Mykolaiv a month in Sloviansk and Kramatorsk

and after the liberation of Kharkiv region, Izyum, Balakliya, Lyman, Kherson region, and Donbas, and a month in Bakhmut.

"You can do anything if you have a lot of money or a lot of time," Kapuscinski's words aptly describe my lifestyle in these areas. I have no car, no money for a hotel or an apartment. Just a camera, two legs, a backpack with a sleeping bag, and random encounters and acquaintances that save you. Civilians in basements, doctors, soldiers, volunteers, and rescuers become your friends, shelter, and food. There is something wild in this, but living with them you stop being a purely journalist, you become a participant in the events, you have a military lifestyle already planted in your head, and you get used to it, and you just live, assimilate to the situation. And in photography, this plays an important role, the role of trust and the naturalness of the moment.
